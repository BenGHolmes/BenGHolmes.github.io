Who I am

Hi, I'm Ben. I'm a third year Engineering Physics student at the University of British Columbia in Vancouver, Canada. I have a passion for physics, software development, and math, and with my endless curiosity and love for problem solving, I hope to build a career in research and academia. I have taken my first step in this direction by pursuing a degree in Engineering Physics, an extremely competitive program that focuses on developing a foundation of high level math and physics knowledge, while also allowing me to customize my degree with a wide range of technical electives.

Recently, my main interest has been in the areas of theoretical physics, computational physics, and astronomy. I've recently taken courses in both quantum mechanics and special relativity, and they have been by far the most enjoyable courses of my degree. I hope to get more experience in physics research over the coming years to help me narrow down my areas of interest and guide my degree moving forwards.

In what little free time I have between classes and extracurricular projects, I'm an avid climber, skier, and backpacker, and I dabble in biking, running and photography.

Projects

Autonomous Rescue Robot with Computer Vision

Course project (June - Aug 2018)

During this past summer, I was lucky enough to participate in the yearly Engineering Physics robot design course. The course is structured as a competition, with the goal being to build a fully autonomous robot that is able to complete the given challenge in just 13 weeks. This year, we had to rescue Ewoks (stuffed animals) from the Empire stronghold by navigating over gaps, differentiating between various infrared light signals, and detecting and capturing 5 stuffed Ewoks along the way.

From the beginning, our team decided that we wanted to try to implement a universal solution, that required no hardcoding or prior knowledge of the course. If successful, our robot would be able to navigate any similar style of course, even if the specific obstacles changed. My main contribution was implementing a computer vision system that allowed us to do exactly that. A Raspberry Pi on the robot continuously captured images of the course ahead using a fisheye camera, and these photos were processed using a Python implementation of the extremely fast YOLO network, running on the Pi. The raspberry pi would then calculate how much the robot needed to turn in order to face the Ewok, and then pass this information to our main STM32 microcontroller, which controlled the motors.
